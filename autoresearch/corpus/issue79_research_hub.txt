## Summary

A comprehensive quant-trader review of the OpenQuant engine, synthesizing findings from three parallel research streams: academic literature (books + papers), AI/mathematical methods for quant trading, and open-source GitHub implementations. The goal: diagnose why the system produces only small profits, and provide a concrete roadmap to institutional-grade performance.

---

## Part 1: Strategy Review — What You Have & Why Profits Are Small

### Current Strategies Assessment

| Strategy | Logic | Verdict |
|----------|-------|---------|
| **Mean Reversion** | Z-score on returns + volume confirmation + SMA-50 trend filter | Sound idea, but the z-score threshold is static; no regime awareness. The SMA-50 filter is binary (on/off) — real quants use continuous regime probabilities. Catches falling knives in trending markets. |
| **Momentum** | EMA crossover + ADX + DI filter | Most crowded retail signal that exists. By the time ADX > 20 and EMAs cross, institutional money has already moved. The signal is **lagging** by construction. |
| **Breakout** | Bollinger squeeze + Donchian + volume surge | Best-constructed strategy. But exit logic has workarounds ("we don't have close directly") and the Chandelier stop uses `%B` as proxy — approximations compound error. |
| **VWAP Reversion** | Mean-reversion to VWAP with session-time weighting | Most interesting — has institutional logic (VWAP as execution benchmark). But session weighting (early/mid/late buckets) is too coarse. |
| **Signal Combiner** | Score-weighted voting | **The #1 architectural problem.** Fixed-weight voting means strategies cancel each other out. Mean-reversion says "buy the dip" while momentum says "sell the trend" — they fight. Without regime-aware weighting, you get signal cancellation → small profits. |
| **Risk** | Kill switch, cost filter, linear/sigmoid sizing, ATR stops, max hold | Good hygiene, but no portfolio-level risk (correlation, sector exposure, drawdown-based deleveraging). ATR is a crude volatility measure. Position sizing has no theoretical grounding (Kelly criterion). |

### Root Cause Diagnosis (Grinold-Kahn Fundamental Law)

The **Fundamental Law of Active Management** states: `IR = IC × √BR × TC`
- **IC** (Information Coefficient / signal quality): Low. Your signals are textbook indicators that every retail quant uses. Alpha is crowded.
- **BR** (Breadth / number of independent bets): Low. You trade single assets with 4 correlated strategies. No pairs, no cross-sectional, no factors.
- **TC** (Transfer Coefficient / implementation efficiency): Unknown but likely mediocre. No execution optimization, basic cost model.

**Your profits are small because IC × √BR is small, and your strategies often cancel each other out (negative TC from signal combiner).**

## Part 2: Critical Gaps to Fill (Prioritized)

### 🔴 Gap 1: Regime Blindness (HIGHEST PRIORITY)

**The Problem:** Running mean-reversion and momentum simultaneously without regime detection causes them to cancel each other. Mean-reversion works in range-bound markets; momentum works in trending markets. Without knowing which regime you're in, your combiner fights itself.

**The Fix — Three Options (increasing sophistication):**

1. **Bayesian Online Changepoint Detection (BOCPD)** — Easiest. The Rust crate [`changepoint`](https://crates.io/crates/changepoint) by Redpoll provides production-grade BOCPD. Detects when market dynamics shift without specifying number of regimes a priori. ~2-4 weeks.
   - Paper: Adams & MacKay (2007) "Bayesian Online Changepoint Detection"

2. **Hidden Markov Model (HMM)** — Standard approach. 2-3 state HMM (bull/bear/sideways) with emissions based on returns + volatility. Online filtering via forward algorithm: O(K²) per step.
   - Paper: Hamilton (1989) "A New Approach to the Economic Analysis of Nonstationary Time Series"
   - Reference impl: [Sakeeb91/market-regime-detection](https://github.com/Sakeeb91/market-regime-detection)
   - Complete system: [0x596173736972/MarketRegimeTrader](https://github.com/0x596173736972/MarketRegimeTrader)

3. **Hidden Semi-Markov Model (HSMM)** — Better regime duration modeling (bull markets last longer than geometric distribution implies).
   - Paper: Bulla & Bulla (2006) "Stylized Facts and Hidden Semi-Markov Models"

**Integration point:** Regime probabilities feed into the Signal Combiner to weight strategies dynamically, and into Risk module to scale position sizes.

---

### 🔴 Gap 2: Static Signal Combiner → Adaptive Strategy Selection

**The Problem:** Fixed score-weighted voting is the worst architectural weakness. Strategy performance is non-stationary.

**The Fix — Three Options:**

1. **Multi-Armed Bandit (Discounted Thompson Sampling)** — Each strategy is an "arm." DS-TS maintains Beta posteriors with exponential discounting, naturally favoring recently-winning strategies. ~200 lines of Rust. ~2-4 weeks.
   - Paper: Raj & Kalyani (2017) "Taming Non-stationary Bandits"
   - Reference: [Bandit Networks for Portfolio Optimization (2024)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.04217)

2. **Meta-Labeling (Lopez de Prado)** — Train a secondary classifier that predicts P(primary signal is profitable). Use Triple Barrier Method for training labels. The meta-model's confidence becomes position size. **Single highest-impact improvement.**
   - Book: Lopez de Prado (2018) "Advances in Financial Machine Learning" Ch. 3, 50
   - Reference impl: [Hudson & Thames meta-labeling](https://github.com/hudson-and-thames/meta-labeling)
   - Feature impl: [FGU1/mlfinlab](https://github.com/FGU1/mlfinlab)

3. **Factor Momentum** — Apply momentum to strategy-level returns: increase weight on recently winning strategies, decrease on losing. Theoretically grounded by Ehsani & Linnainmaa (2022).

### 🟡 Gap 3: Position Sizing Has No Theoretical Foundation

**The Problem:** Linear sizing ignores signal quality. Sigmoid is heuristic. Neither is mathematically optimal.

**The Fix — Kelly Criterion:**

Replace with **fractional Kelly** (half-Kelly is standard practice due to estimation error):
```
f* = (p × b - q) / b
```
where p = win probability, b = win/loss ratio, estimated from rolling strategy performance.

- Paper: Kelly (1956), Thorp (2006) "The Kelly Criterion in Blackjack, Sports Betting, and the Stock Market"
- **Regime-conditional Kelly:** In high-vol regimes (detected via Gap 1), reduce to quarter-Kelly. In low-vol, use half-Kelly.
- Paper: "Sizing the Risk: Kelly, VIX, and Hybrid Approaches" (2025, [arXiv:2508.16598](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.16598))

---

### 🟡 Gap 4: Crude Volatility Estimation (ATR → GARCH)

**The Problem:** ATR is a moving average of absolute ranges — it ignores volatility clustering and asymmetry (negative shocks increase vol more than positive).

**The Fix — GARCH(1,1) minimum, GJR-GARCH ideal:**

```
σ²_t = ω + α × r²_{t-1} + β × σ²_{t-1}           [GARCH]
σ²_t = ω + (α + γ × I_{r<0}) × r²_{t-1} + β × σ²_{t-1}  [GJR-GARCH, adds leverage effect]
```

- Only 3-4 parameters, computationally cheap, perfect for Rust
- Papers: Engle (1982), Bollerslev (1986), Glosten-Jagannathan-Runkle (1993)
- **Impact:** Better stop-loss distances, better position sizing, regime signal input
- Reference impl: [Hupperich-Manuel/GARCH_Model_Strategy](https://github.com/Hupperich-Manuel/GARCH_Model_Startegy)

---

### 🟡 Gap 5: No Portfolio-Level Optimization

**The Problem:** Strategies are combined via signal voting, not portfolio optimization. No consideration of correlation between strategies, no risk budgeting, no drawdown-based deleveraging.

**The Fix — Add a Portfolio Construction Layer:**

- **Hierarchical Risk Parity (HRP)** — Doesn't require covariance matrix inversion (robust with few assets/strategies). Allocates risk budget across strategies by diversification benefit.
- **Risk Parity** — Each strategy contributes equal risk, not equal capital.
- Libraries:
  - [skfolio](https://github.com/skfolio/skfolio) — Most complete, scikit-learn API, peer-reviewed (2025)
  - [Riskfolio-Lib](https://github.com/dcajasn/Riskfolio-Lib) — 24 risk measures, Kelly optimization
  - [PyPortfolioOpt](https://github.com/PyPortfolio/PyPortfolioOpt) — Simpler, great docs
- Books: Scherer "Portfolio Construction and Risk Budgeting"; Grinold & Kahn "Active Portfolio Management"

---

### 🟢 Gap 6: No Pairs Trading / Statistical Arbitrage (New Alpha Source)

**The Problem:** All current strategies are directional single-asset. Breadth (BR in fundamental law) is low. Adding market-neutral strategies diversifies alpha.

**The Fix — Cointegration-Based Pairs Trading:**

1. Test pairs for cointegration (Engle-Granger or Johansen)
2. Model spread as Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process: `dS = θ(μ - S)dt + σdW`
3. Trade spread when it deviates > N std devs from equilibrium
4. Dynamic hedge ratio via Kalman Filter

- Paper: Gatev et al. (2006) "Pairs Trading: Performance of a Relative-Value Arbitrage Rule"
- Survey: Krauss (2017) "Statistical Arbitrage Pairs Trading Strategies: Review and Outlook"
- Reference impl: [akhil2706/PairsTrading](https://github.com/akhil2706/PairsTrading)
- Rust OU process: [avhz/RustQuant](https://github.com/avhz/RustQuant) has OU implementation

---

### 🟢 Gap 7: No Cross-Sectional Momentum / Factor Models

**The Problem:** OpenQuant's momentum is time-series only (EMA crossover on a single asset). Cross-sectional momentum (ranking assets by relative performance) is a fundamentally different and more robust signal.

**The Fix:**

- Rank universe of assets by trailing 2-12 month returns (skip most recent month for reversal)
- Long top decile, short/avoid bottom decile
- Paper: Jegadeesh & Titman (1993) "Returns to Buying Winners and Selling Losers"
- Factor attribution: Fama-French 5-factor model to understand what risk you're actually taking
- Reference impl: [jerryxyx/AlphaTrading](https://github.com/jerryxyx/AlphaTrading)

---

### 🟢 Gap 8: Execution Intelligence

**The Problem:** Likely using immediate market orders. Even for retail size, slippage compounds.

**The Fix:**

- Model your own market impact: `impact ≈ σ × (Q/V)^0.6` (Almgren et al., 2005)
- Use limit orders with timeout fallback to market
- Split larger orders across bars
- Paper: Almgren & Chriss (2000) "Optimal Execution of Portfolio Transactions"
- Book: Barry Johnson "Algorithmic Trading and DMA"
- For realistic backtesting: [nkaz001/hftbacktest](https://github.com/nkaz001/hftbacktest) (Rust+Python, models latency and queue position)

## TL;DR — The Three Things That Will Move the Needle Most

1. **Add regime detection (BOCPD/HMM)** and wire it to the signal combiner. Your strategies fight each other without this. This is likely the single biggest reason profits are small.

2. **Replace the static signal combiner** with adaptive weighting (Thompson Sampling or meta-labeling). Fixed weights in a non-stationary market guarantee mediocrity.

3. **Add pairs trading** for a market-neutral alpha source. All your current strategies are directional — you need uncorrelated return streams to increase Breadth in the fundamental law.

Everything else is optimization. These three are structural.
