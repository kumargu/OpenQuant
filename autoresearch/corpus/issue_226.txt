[Research] Q1 2026 regime analysis — why Jan/Feb lost money, Mar profitable

## Question

Our S&P 500 same-sector pairs trading strategy (2-5 day half-lives) went from 29% win rate / -$1,373 in January to 63% win rate / +$2,375 in March. Same engine, same entry/exit logic. Why?

| Period | Pairs | Win rate | P&L |
|---|---|---|---|
| Jan 19 - Feb 1 | 14 | 29% | -$1,373 |
| Feb 2 - 15 | 20 | 45% | -$2,067 |
| Feb 16 - Mar 1 | 22 | 46% | -$1,719 |
| Mar 2 - 15 | 24 | 49% | -$308 |
| Mar 13 - 27 | 41 | 63% | +$2,375 |

## Findings

### 1. Q1 2026 Market Timeline — Three Distinct Regimes

**January 2026: Tariff uncertainty + DeepSeek AI shock + earnings season**
- Jan 20: Trump tariff fears spike VIX above 20, S&P 500 drops 2.1% in one day
- Jan 27: DeepSeek AI model release causes tech selloff — Nasdaq -3.07%, Nvidia -13.5%
- Sector rotation OUT of tech into defensives (Dow +0.65% same day Nasdaq -3.07%)
- Q4 2025 earnings season running Jan 16 - Feb 15, with concentrated surprises in IT, Industrials, Communications
- CAPE ratio hit 40.2 in January — highest since dot-com era
- **Regime: HIGH DISPERSION + SECTOR ROTATION → same-sector pairs decouple**

**February 2026: SCOTUS tariff ruling + continued earnings + policy whiplash**
- Feb 20: Supreme Court strikes down Trump's IEEPA tariffs → market rallies 0.7%
- Same day: Trump announces 10% global tariff under Section 122 → market seesaws
- Average effective tariff rate: 15.8% (up from 2.3% at end of 2024)
- Sector rotation: Energy, Materials, Consumer Staples outperform; Tech, Financials, Discretionary underperform
- Feb 28: US-Israel joint airstrikes on Iran begin ("Operation Epic Fury")
- **Regime: POLICY SHOCK + SECTOR ROTATION → pairs in wrong sectors decouple**

**March 2026: Iran war + oil shock + massive sector divergence**
- Brent crude surges 36% (Feb 27 → Mar 27), Dubai physical oil +76%
- Strait of Hormuz traffic drops to near-zero; 10 mb/d production cut
- Energy sector: +18.2% MTD by Mar 24 (ConocoPhillips +21.3%, Exxon +16.4%)
- Defense/Industrials: +14.7% (Raytheon +22.1%, Lockheed +19.4%)
- S&P 500 overall: -2% MTD — enormous CROSS-sector dispersion
- **But INTRA-sector correlation likely INCREASED** — within Energy, all names moved together on oil. Within Defense, all names moved together on war spending. Within other sectors, all names fell together on recession fears
- **Regime: HIGH CROSS-SECTOR DISPERSION + HIGH INTRA-SECTOR CORRELATION → same-sector pairs CONVERGE**

### 2. The Key Insight: Intra-Sector vs Cross-Sector Dispersion

Our strategy trades SAME-SECTOR pairs. The critical variable is not overall market volatility but **intra-sector dispersion**:

- **January-February**: Multiple idiosyncratic shocks (DeepSeek hit AI stocks unevenly, earnings surprises varied within sectors, tariff impact varied by company supply chain exposure). This creates HIGH INTRA-SECTOR DISPERSION → spreads trend instead of mean-reverting → pairs trading loses.

- **March**: One dominant macro shock (Iran war / oil prices) that affected entire sectors uniformly. Energy stocks all rose together. Consumer stocks all fell together on recession fears. This creates LOW INTRA-SECTOR DISPERSION → spreads stay tight and mean-revert → pairs trading wins.

**This is the dispersion paradox for pairs trading**: our strategy profits when the market is chaotic at the sector level but orderly WITHIN sectors.

### 3. Specific Mechanisms of January/February Losses

**Earnings-driven decoupling**: Q4 2025 earnings ran from Jan 16 to ~Feb 15. An earnings surprise for one member of a pair but not the other causes a fundamental gap that will NOT mean-revert. The ADF test on historical data cannot predict an upcoming earnings surprise. With 2-5 day half-lives, our pairs are fully exposed to earnings gaps.

**DeepSeek shock (Jan 27)**: Nvidia -13.5% but AMD reactions differed. Within the semiconductor sector, stocks with different AI exposure diverged. Within financials, those with AI-heavy trading operations diverged from traditional banks. This is intra-sector dispersion from a single catalyst.

**Tariff heterogeneity**: Within Consumer Discretionary, a retailer importing from China was hit differently than a services company. Within Industrials, a defense contractor benefited while a manufacturer with global supply chains suffered. Same sector, opposite moves.

### 4. Specific Mechanisms of March Profits

**Oil as a universal sector sorter**: The Iran war created a single dominant factor. Within Energy, ALL producers benefited (correlation → 1). Within Consumer Staples, ALL companies faced higher input costs. Within Financials, ALL banks faced the same macro uncertainty. This uniformity within sectors is exactly what makes same-sector pairs mean-revert.

**Increased number of pairs (41 vs 14)**: The pair-picker found more cointegrated pairs in March — likely BECAUSE sectors were moving in lockstep, making cointegration easier to detect. This is a positive feedback loop: the regime that favors pairs trading also makes it easier to find pairs.

### 5. Academic Support

- **Gatev et al. (2006)**: Pairs trading profits are concentrated in "convergence" periods. Their excess returns declined when tested on later, more efficient markets — consistent with regime dependence.
- **Do and Faff (2010)**: Found pairs trading is less profitable overall but "continues to generate good profits during market downturns" — consistent with our March 2026 experience during the Iran war market downturn.
- **Yang et al. (2016)**: Markov regime-switching model for pairs trading on S&P 500 outperforms static approaches, especially during the 2008 crisis — supporting the idea that regime conditioning improves performance.
- **BIS (2008)**: "Evaluating correlation breakdowns during periods of market volatility" — volatility spikes correspond to HIGH inter-sector correlation (sectors move together) but this paper does not distinguish intra-sector dispersion.
- **Krauss (2017)**: Notes that pairs trading "underperforms under volatile or distressed market conditions because selected asset pairs fail to revert to equilibrium within the investment horizon" — but this is about CROSS-pair volatility, not controlling for intra-sector correlation.

### 6. Literature Gap: Intra-Sector Dispersion as Regime Filter

Most papers study VIX or overall market volatility as the regime variable. But for SAME-SECTOR pairs, the relevant variable is **intra-sector dispersion** — the standard deviation of returns within each sector. This is measurable and filterable:

- Compute daily returns for all S&P 500 stocks in each sector
- Compute rolling standard deviation of cross-sectional returns within each sector
- When intra-sector dispersion is HIGH → reduce position size or skip entries
- When intra-sector dispersion is LOW → normal or increased position size

This is distinct from VIX filtering and more targeted for our strategy.

### 7. Existing Engine Regime Detection vs What We Need

**What we have** (pair-picker `regime.rs`):
- Rolling volatility of the SPREAD between two specific stocks
- Classifies into Calm/Normal/Volatile based on spread vol
- Per-pair, backward-looking

**What we have** (engine `features/regime.rs`):
- BOCPD changepoint detection on individual returns
- Vol percentile tracker
- Classifies into LowVol/Normal/HighVol/Crisis
- Global, not sector-specific

**What we need**:
- **Intra-sector dispersion tracker**: rolling cross-sectional std of returns within each S&P sector
- **Earnings calendar awareness**: binary flag for "any constituent in this pair reports earnings within N days"
- **VIX level as secondary filter**: VIX > 25 with rising intra-sector dispersion = danger zone

## Recommendation

### Immediate (no code change needed)
1. **Earnings blackout**: Do not enter new pairs within 3 trading days of either constituent's earnings date. This alone would have avoided most January losses. Can be implemented in the pair-picker scoring or as a filter in the Python orchestration layer.

### Short-term (engine changes)
2. **Intra-sector dispersion gate**: Add a rolling 20-day cross-sectional dispersion measure per sector. When dispersion > 75th percentile of its own history, reduce position size by 50% for pairs in that sector. This is a direct, measurable regime filter for our specific strategy type.

3. **VIX regime overlay**: When VIX > 25 AND rising (5-day slope > 0), apply 0.5x position size multiplier. This catches systemic stress without being too aggressive in cutting exposure.

### Medium-term (research + implementation)
4. **Regime-switching model for spread dynamics**: Per Yang et al. (2016), fit a Markov-switching OU process to each spread. In the "trending" regime, do not trade. In the "mean-reverting" regime, trade normally. This is more sophisticated than the current static OU half-life.

5. **Conditional entry_z**: Widen entry_z to 2.5 sigma (from current ~2.0) when intra-sector dispersion is elevated. This requires stronger signals to enter, filtering out false entries that would fail to converge.
